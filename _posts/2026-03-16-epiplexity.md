# From Entropy to Epiplexity: Why Shannon Information Theory Misleads Us About Learning

*A deep dive into a new information measure — epiplexity — that separates learnable structure from incompressible noise, and explains paradoxes that classical information theory cannot.*

---

Shannon information theory has been the backbone of how we think about data, compression, and communication for over 75 years. But a new paper by Marc Finzi, Shikai Qiu, Yiding Jiang, Pavel Izmailov, J. Zico Kolter, and Andrew Gordon Wilson — [*"From Entropy to Epiplexity"*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.03220) — argues that when it comes to **learning**, Shannon's framework is fundamentally misleading.

The core problem: Shannon entropy treats all bits equally. A random bit and a structured bit count the same. But for a learner with limited compute — like a neural network, or a human brain — these are wildly different. One carries learnable structure, the other is just noise. The paper introduces **epiplexity** (epistemic complexity) to formalize this distinction.

---

## Three Paradoxes That Break Classical Information Theory

The paper builds its case around three observations that seem paradoxical under standard information theory but are perfectly natural once you account for computational constraints.

### Paradox 1: Deterministic Transformations Can't Create Information... But They Do

Classical theory says that a deterministic function $f$ can never increase information: $K(f(x)) \leq K(x) + K(f)$. The output is always "simpler" than the input plus the description of the function. This is a theorem — it's true.

And yet: AlphaZero starts from random play and, through purely deterministic self-play, generates chess strategies that took humanity centuries to discover. Synthetic data pipelines create training examples that improve model performance. How?

**The resolution:** Classical information theory assumes unbounded computation. A cryptographically secure pseudorandom number generator (CSPRNG) maps a short seed $k$ to a long string $G(U_k)$ that *looks* random to any polynomial-time observer. In classical terms, the output has low Kolmogorov complexity (it's just "run $G$ on seed $k$"). But no efficient algorithm can distinguish it from true randomness — so for any bounded learner, the output carries genuinely new entropy.

The paper formalizes this: for a CSPRNG with advantage $\varepsilon(k)$,

$$H_{\text{Poly}}(G(U_k)) > H_{\text{Poly}}(U_k) + n - n\varepsilon(k) - k - O(1)$$

Deterministic computation **does** create information — time-bounded information. The illusion that it can't comes from assuming observers have unlimited compute.

### Paradox 2: Data Ordering Shouldn't Matter... But It Does

Shannon entropy $H(X, Y) = H(Y, X)$. Kolmogorov complexity is symmetric up to a constant: $K(x, y) = K(y, x) + O(\log n)$. Information content doesn't depend on which direction you read the data.

But anyone who has trained a language model knows: text processed left-to-right yields dramatically better models than text processed right-to-left (or randomly shuffled). Curriculum learning — feeding easy examples before hard ones — consistently helps. If information is order-independent, why?

**The resolution:** In the time-bounded setting, computing $G$ forward might be easy (polynomial time), but inverting $G^{-1}$ might be intractable. The symmetry $K(G) = K(G^{-1})$ holds with unbounded compute, but $K_T(G) \neq K_T(G^{-1})$ in general. Data ordering determines which structures become learnable within a fixed compute budget. The directionality is real — it's just invisible to classical information theory.

### Paradox 3: Likelihood Models Can't Beat the Data Generator... But They Can

If your data comes from distribution $P^*$, then $P^*$ minimizes expected log-loss: $\mathbb{E}_{P^*}[-\log P(X)] \geq \mathbb{E}_{P^*}[-\log P^*(X)]$ for all $P$. No model should do better than the true generator. So likelihood models should, at best, replicate the generator — never discovering anything "new."

But this is obviously wrong in practice. Conway's Game of Life is generated by trivial local rules on a grid. Yet trained models discover gliders, oscillators, and complex emergent structures that are nowhere in the generating rule set. Language models discover grammar, semantics, and reasoning from next-token prediction on web text.

**The resolution:** The generating process may be simple (constant-time per step), but a polynomial-time observer can discover hierarchical patterns that compress the data more efficiently than the generator itself. The model isn't beating the generator at its own game — it's playing a different game, one where bounded computation discovers emergent structure that the simple generator never represented explicitly.

## What Is Epiplexity?

The central definition is elegant. Given a time-bounded model class $\mathcal{P}_T$ (programs that run in time $T$), find the optimal program $P^*$ minimizing:

$$|P| + \mathbb{E}[\log \frac{1}{P(X)}]$$

This is a minimum description length (MDL) decomposition. The first term is the program's length; the second is how well it compresses the data. The optimal $P^*$ gives us two quantities:

- **Epiplexity** $S_T(X) = |P^*|$ — the length of the optimal program. This is the **learnable structural content** of the data.
- **Time-Bounded Entropy** $H_T(X) = \mathbb{E}[\log \frac{1}{P^*(X)}]$ — the residual loss. This is the **unlearnable random content**.

The key insight: classical entropy conflates these two. A dataset of random noise and a dataset of structured text might have the same Shannon entropy, but they have completely different epiplexity. The structured text has high epiplexity (lots of learnable patterns) and moderate time-bounded entropy. The noise has near-zero epiplexity and maximal time-bounded entropy.

---

## CSPRNGs: The Perfect Litmus Test

The paper validates epiplexity against a known ground truth: cryptographically secure pseudorandom generators. We *know* CSPRNG output has structure (it's generated from a short seed) but that structure is computationally inaccessible to efficient observers. The theory should predict: **high time-bounded entropy, near-zero epiplexity.**

And it does. Theorem 9 shows:

$$S_T(G(U_k)) \leq c + n\varepsilon(k) \approx 0$$

$$H_T(G(U_k)) \approx n$$

CSPRNG output has essentially no learnable structure and maximal (time-bounded) randomness. Classical Kolmogorov complexity would say the opposite — the output is highly compressible (just store the seed). Epiplexity gets it right; classical theory gets it wrong.

## Cellular Automata: A Beautiful Illustration

The paper uses Wolfram's elementary cellular automata to demonstrate the three types of data:

| Rule | Behavior | Epiplexity $S_T$ | Time-Bounded Entropy $H_T$ |
|------|----------|------|------|
| Rule 15 | Stable/repetitive | Low | Low |
| Rule 30 | Chaotic | Low | High |
| Rule 54 | Complex | **High** | Moderate |

**Rule 15** produces repetitive, trivially predictable patterns — low on both dimensions. **Rule 30** produces chaotic output that is essentially pseudorandom — high entropy but nothing to learn. **Rule 54** is the interesting one: it produces complex, structured patterns (like Class IV automata generally do) that contain genuine learnable information.

When you train neural networks on these three rules, the loss curves tell the story: Rule 15 converges instantly, Rule 30 barely improves beyond random guessing, and Rule 54 shows sustained improvement — the model keeps discovering new structure. That sustained improvement *is* the epiplexity being extracted.

## Measuring Epiplexity in Practice

The definition is clean but not directly computable (it requires optimizing over all time-bounded programs). The paper proposes two practical approximations:

### Prequential Coding

Train a model sequentially on a data stream, accumulating the loss reduction over time. The epiplexity is approximately the **area under the loss curve above the final loss**:

$$|P_{\text{preq}}| \approx \sum_i \left(\log \frac{1}{P_i(Z_i)} - \log \frac{1}{P_M(Z_i)}\right)$$

This is computationally cheap — you just need a training run and its loss curve.

### Requential Coding

Use a trained "teacher" model to generate synthetic data, then train a "student" from scratch on it. The epiplexity is the **area between the teacher and student loss curves**:

$$|P_{\text{req}}| = \sum_i \text{KL}(P_i^t \| P_i^s)$$

This is more rigorous (it gives explicit runtime guarantees) but 2-10x more expensive.

Both methods correlate well in practice, and the paper shows they produce consistent rankings across datasets.

## Why This Matters: Data Selection Over Model Selection

The deepest practical implication of epiplexity is a shift in focus from **model selection** to **data selection**. Classical MDL tells you which model to pick for a given dataset. Epiplexity tells you which **dataset** to pick for a given compute budget.

This is exactly the problem that matters in the era of large-scale pretraining. Everyone is using roughly the same architectures (Transformers). The differentiator is data — its quality, its ordering, its composition. Epiplexity provides a principled, task-agnostic measure of how much learnable structure a dataset contains.

The paper shows that epiplexity rankings across datasets correlate with downstream task performance — even for tasks the model was never trained on. High-epiplexity data produces models that generalize better, not because it teaches specific skills, but because it contains more extractable structure per compute dollar.

---

## Scaling Behavior

Under standard neural scaling law assumptions:

- **Epiplexity grows with compute budget $T$**: more compute lets you discover more structure.
- **Time-bounded entropy decreases with $T$**: more compute lets you predict more of the "random" component.
- **Per-token entropy decreases with dataset size**: larger datasets have more redundancy to exploit.

This aligns with the empirical observation that scaling compute and data simultaneously produces the best models — you're increasing both the amount of structure available (more data → more epiplexity) and the ability to extract it (more compute → lower time-bounded entropy).

## Key Takeaways

1. **Shannon entropy is the wrong measure for learning.** It conflates learnable structure with incompressible noise. Two datasets with the same entropy can have wildly different learning value.

2. **Computation creates information.** This sounds impossible under classical theory, but it's perfectly natural when you account for bounded compute. AlphaZero, synthetic data, and data augmentation all exploit this.

3. **Data ordering matters — formally.** Not just as a training heuristic, but as a fundamental property of time-bounded information. Curriculum learning has theoretical backing.

4. **Models can discover structure the data generator never represented.** Emergence isn't just a buzzword — it's a consequence of polynomial-time observers finding patterns that constant-time generators can't.

5. **Epiplexity is a foundation for data-centric AI.** As the field shifts from "which model" to "which data," having a principled measure of data quality that doesn't require downstream evaluation could be transformative.

---

## Final Thoughts

This paper does something rare: it identifies fundamental limitations in a framework we've been using for decades and proposes a replacement that is both theoretically grounded and practically measurable. The three paradoxes are compelling, the resolution is elegant, and the experimental validation — from cellular automata to chess to natural language — is thorough.

The biggest open question is how epiplexity measurements will scale to frontier model training. The measurement procedures require training runs, which are expensive at scale. But even approximate rankings could be enormously valuable for data curation decisions that currently rely on heuristics and intuition.

If you work on data selection, curriculum learning, or synthetic data generation, this paper is essential reading. It provides the theoretical vocabulary for what practitioners have long felt intuitively: not all bits are created equal.
